<p>Most outbound starts with one assumption: the email reaches the inbox. If it does not, nothing else in your campaign matters. An email that lands in spam is never opened, and a prospect who never sees your message is not part of your campaign at all.</p>
<p>Here is what email deliverability actually is, why open rate follows from it, the rules the major providers now enforce, and the factors you control to keep both high.</p>
<h2>What email deliverability is</h2>
<p>Email deliverability is whether a sent email reaches the recipient&#39;s inbox, not just their mail server. There is a real difference between an email being accepted by the mailbox and being placed in the inbox. High delivery with low deliverability means your mail is arriving but landing in spam, promotions, or junk. Inbox placement is what drives your open rate.</p>
<h2>Why open rate follows deliverability</h2>
<p>Say you target 1000 prospects at a 30 percent open rate. Only 300 people read your message, and only those 300 can reply. Push the open rate to 55 percent and, all else equal, you roughly double the replies from the same list. Open rate is the first number to watch, as long as you are not inflating it with clickbait subject lines. Do not settle for 30 percent when 40 to 70 percent is normal once the fundamentals are right.</p>
<h2>The rules you have to meet now</h2>
<p>Since this topic was first written, the major providers have turned deliverability from a best practice into a hard requirement. If you send to Gmail, Yahoo, or Outlook addresses, you now need to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Authenticate with SPF and DKIM, and publish a DMARC record, with your From domain aligned to one of them.</li>
<li>Offer one-click unsubscribe on marketing email and honor it within two days.</li>
<li>Keep your spam complaint rate under 0.3 percent, with under 0.1 percent as the real target. You can watch it in Google Postmaster Tools and Yahoo Sender Hub.</li>
</ul>
<p>These apply in full to anyone sending more than 5000 emails a day to consumer inboxes, and the authentication parts are worth doing at any volume. Non-compliant mail is now rejected outright, not just filtered to spam.</p>
<h2>The factors you control</h2>
<p><strong>Authentication and technical setup.</strong> SPF lists which servers may send mail for your domain. DKIM signs your messages so the recipient can verify they were not altered in transit. DMARC ties the two together and tells receiving servers what to do when a message fails. Without these, your mail is far more likely to be flagged or refused.</p>
<p><strong>Data quality.</strong> The data you send to directly affects deliverability. Relevant, accurate, validated contacts keep your open rates up and your bounces down. Sending to wrong or invalid addresses hurts your domain reputation quickly.</p>
<p><strong>Sender reputation.</strong> Mailbox providers score your sending domain from 0 to 100. Opens and replies raise it; spam complaints, spam traps, and high bounce rates lower it. A poor reputation sends your mail to spam or gets it rejected.</p>
<p><strong>Bounce rate.</strong> Keep it under 5 percent. Hard bounces, which are sends to wrong or non-existent addresses, damage your domain reputation with every attempt. Ask your data provider for their expected bounce rate, and pause a campaign if it climbs past 10 percent.</p>
<p><strong>Sending volume.</strong> Send 25 to 150 emails per day per mailbox, with uneven gaps between sends, so your sending looks human rather than automated. To scale, add more mailboxes rather than sending more from one. Favor high conversion from low volume over the reverse.</p>
<p><strong>Content.</strong> Your subject line should say in a few words what your message says in a hundred, without baiting the reader into opening it. Your message should start a conversation, not try to close a deal. Personalization, done with real research rather than a single first-name token, is what earns both the open and the reply.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Deliverability is the foundation everything else sits on. Get your authentication, data, reputation, and sending discipline right, and your open and reply rates follow. The providers now enforce the basics, so the cost of ignoring them is no longer a lower open rate, it is mail that never arrives.</p>
